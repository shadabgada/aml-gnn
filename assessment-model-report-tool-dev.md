*From: Module Guide Master Project 2025-2026, DDB Master — Amsterdam University of Applied Sciences*

## C.3.2 Expected Content of Tool Development Reports

### Chapter 1 — Introduction

- Critical synthesis of business problems and research objectives in a problem statement.
- Translation of problems and objectives into main research question.
- Decomposition of main research question into sub-questions, making sure these are answerable, feasible, relevant (given the problem statement), interconnected/coherent, clear/simple, and reasonable in number.
- Brief summary of preferred theoretical angle and methodological approach. You will discuss this in more detail in Chapter 2 (theoretical framework/literature review) and Chapter 3 (research methodology) but it is good to already let the reader know what to expect.
- Contribution to the practice community (relating back to the research objectives) and if possible, also academic contribution.
- Brief description of the report structure.

### Chapter 2 — Theoretical Framework / Literature Review

- Critical synthesis of relevant management and/or computer science perspectives from the literature (in the form of theories, variables and techniques/approaches) to identify (where relevant):
  - The current state of the literature with regard to the subject matter;
  - Desired variables to be processed and generated in the tool;
  - Desired process to be designed in the tool;
  - Performance indicators to test and verify the tool.

### Chapter 3 — Research Methodology and Tool Development

- Elaboration of the developed tool with regard to (where applicable):
  - Algorithm;
  - Database;
  - Computational and/or forecasting elements.
- Justification of the choices made in the tool's design and development based on the theories, variables and techniques/approaches discussed in Chapter 2 with an explicit comparison of advantages and disadvantages of multiple tool design options. It can also be the case that you do not end up with one single design choice for the tool in this chapter, but at least here, the options should already be narrowed down to only a few options.
- Justification of the efforts made to conduct the research (including the development of the tool) ethically and, in the case of measurements, to ensure reliability and validity.

### Chapter 4 — Results, Analyses and Tool Performance

- Interpretation of the tool's output.
- Investigation of the tool's effectiveness and reliability:
  - At least the tool can be tested in different scenarios, potentially based on different design options (see Chapter 3). For example, when developing a machine learning algorithm, using different algorithms which are then tested with different training and testing datasets;
  - Preferably, the student should also take into account the tool's potential scalability, e.g., test whether it can also work with a larger (simulated) dataset;
  - Preferably, the student should also take into account the tool's generalizability, e.g., test whether it can also work with different (simulated) datasets.

### Chapter 5 (or additional chapters) — Discussion, Recommendations and Conclusions

- Conclusions that are justified based on the interpreted results and/or the performance of the developed tool. When multiple design options were considered, here you can recommend the most appropriate alternative that could be implemented.
- Explicit answers to the research (sub-)questions based on the content of the previous chapters.
- Recommendations that are intended to aid the focal organization and a larger part of the industry or society.
- When possible (not required, but this could lead to a higher grade): theoretical implications.
- A critical yet nuanced overview of the study's limitations and suggestions for future research.
- Documentation of the tool's requirements and development. Preferably, the tool's replicability and scalability should be taken into account. This documentation should be included in the report, for example as an appendix.

## Grade Calculation

The overall report grade is a weighted average of the five graded criteria:

| Criterion                                   | Weight |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------ |
| Problem statement and research questions    | 10%    |
| Theoretical framework                       | 10%    |
| Research methodology and tool development   | 30%    |
| Results, analyses and tool performance      | 20%    |
| Discussion, recommendations and conclusions | 30%    |

**Critical rule:** If any graded criterion scores below 5.5, the overall grade is capped at that insufficient grade — the lowest failing grade becomes the final grade, regardless of the weighted average.
